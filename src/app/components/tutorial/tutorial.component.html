Searching promiscuous proteins in ProtMiscuity

There are many ways to find your protein in ProtMiscuity. The search is set by default to protein name. The user should specify a UniProt ID or a common protein name in the the search field and click the search button. After the search, ProtMiscuity will show the results page with one card per protein. The user can press the desired card to retrieve all the information available for the protein.

Alternative search
  • By organism

    • The user can also search for all proteins related with the organism of interest by selecting 'Organism' from the drop-down menu

  • By reaction

    • The user can also search for all the proteins having this promiscuous or canonic reaction.

After a search, the results page shows a card where you can press in order to get the all the information available of this protein.

Visualizing protein data

All the information is collapsed and listed in five items in which the user can search for the specific information of interest: general information, sequence and active sites information, structural information, including conformational diversity, and external links.

Example
General Information

The general information section includes: the protein's common name, the UNIPROT (The UniProt Consortium, 2015) and the KEGG IDs (Kanehisa et al., 2016), the organism from which the protein comes from and the protein family name.

Example
Sequence and active sites

This information is placed in context by residue-level information mapped from major databases of protein domains, secondary and tertiary structures, intrinsic disorder and binding sites, all provided by ProViz (Jehl et al.,2016).

Example
Reactions and Kinetics

ProtMiscuity provides information on the canonic and/or promiscuous reactions catalyzed by the protein. Clicking on a reaction card shows the reaction substrates and products, linked to PubChem (Kim et al., 2016) and with kinetic parameters (Km, kcat) when available.

Structures visualization

Structural visualization of all PDB ​structures (Berman et al., 2000) related with the protein of interest is performed with 3Dmol.js (Rego et al., 2014).
